Credits and debits
A bank statement lists all the money that goes in and out of your account. The money that goes in is called a credit, like when your workplace deposits your paycheck—that's a good thing because you have more money to use. On January 3rd, you got a credit from GadgetWorks Payroll for
\[\$1{,}000.00\]
.
The money that comes out is called a debit. Paying bills or taking out cash are debits; they use up some of your money. For example, on January 2nd, you paid BrightSpark Electric
\[\$45.00\]
, which is a debit.
At the end of the statement, the final amount of money you have is what's left after all the credits and debits are completed.

Balancing a checkbook
In the past, people would keep track of their money by "balancing a checkbook," which meant carefully checking their records against the bank's records to make sure everything matched up. It involved writing down each check you wrote and each deposit you made, then adding or subtracting those figures from your balance. By comparing this with the bank statement, you could figure out exactly how much money you had and catch any mistakes or unauthorized transactions.
Nowadays, "balancing a checkbook" has been replaced with reviewing your online bank statement or using a mobile app. Digital tools automatically track every deposit, payment, and purchase as they happen, and your current balance is always visible. You can instantly check your account activity, anytime and anywhere.
Steps to checking your account
Look at your statement every week. This helps you catch mistakes fast.
Make sure your paychecks and bill payments are happening right. You don't want to miss a payment or not get the money you earned. Set up your bill payments to happen just after you get paid. That way, you know you have enough money to cover them. If you know your paycheck comes in on the 1st, you might set up your bill payments for the 3rd.
Keep an eye on the "pending" stuff. Remember, that money will soon be gone from your account.
Check for fees. Know why your bank charged them and see if you can avoid them next time.
Watch how much you spend. Even small buys can add up, so keep track to avoid surprises.
If you see a transaction you do not recognize, contact your bank, as it could be fraud.
Conclusion
Checking on your bank account often keeps you in charge of your money. It’s like knowing exactly what’s in your wallet, so you never run out at the wrong time. With a little bit of attention, you can avoid money troubles and feel good about your bank balance.
