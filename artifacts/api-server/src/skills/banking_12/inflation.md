Inflation and your wallet
Have you ever heard your grandparents say, "Back in my day, a candy bar cost just a nickel"? Well, that's inflation for you! Inflation means that over time, the prices of things we buy go up. But, not everything gets more expensive over time: some things actually get cheaper. Read to find out more!
Understanding inflation
Inflation means the prices of things we buy are generally going up over time, making your money worth less. While the cost of living tends to climb, not everything gets more expensive at the same rate, and some things even become cheaper, making inflation that much more complicated to calculate.
How do we measure inflation?
To measure inflation, experts use the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which is like a big shopping list of items that average people spend their money on, including food, housing, and transport. The CPI tracks how the prices of these items change from one year to the next.
As an example, if this list costs
\[\$100\]
one year and then
\[\$102\]
the next, the increase is
\[\$2\]
or
\[2\%\]
. That's what we mean when we talk about the inflation rate.
But figuring out CPI isn't as straightforward as you might think. When you hear that the inflation rate is
\[2\%\]
, it means on average, prices have risen by that amount. However, while some prices go up, others can actually drop.
When prices fall
Take flat-screen TVs, for example. When they first hit the market, buying one might have set you back thousands of dollars. Now, you can pick one up for a few hundred dollars. This drop in price is thanks to advances in technology and manufacturing, which make it cheaper to produce these TVs.
Other examples of items that have become more affordable over time include computers, smartphones, LED light bulbs, online services, etc.

Inflation and your savings
Imagine saving up a million dollars for retirement, thinking you'll be comfortable. But with inflation, especially if your savings don't grow, what feels like a lot now might just cover the basics in the future. This is where the inflation rate comes into play. If your savings aren't growing at least as fast as inflation, you might find yourself coming up short when it comes to big expenses or maintaining your lifestyle in retirement.
Why does CPI or inflation matter?
The CPI matters because it's a key indicator of how the economy is doing. It affects decisions made by governments, businesses, and individuals:
Governments use it to adjust social security and pension payments, known as cost-of-living adjustments.
Businesses consider it when setting prices of things they sell.
Employers use the CPI to decide if they need to give their workers a raise to match the rising cost of living. So, it's a good idea for you to keep an eye on how your pay changes compared to the CPI. This way, you can make sure your money still goes as far as it needs to for your expenses, and can judge how big of a raise you actually got.
Individuals can use it to understand how inflation is affecting their , helping them make decisions about spending and saving.
To sum it up
Understanding inflation is important because it shows you how the value of your money changes over time. Even if you're earning interest on your savings, inflation can decrease the worth of that money, meaning what you can buy with it might become less and less. By understanding both interest and inflation, you can make smarter decisions, like where to keep your savings so they don't lose value, ensuring your money works harder for you and helps maintain or even improve your lifestyle over time.

Inflation and real interest rate
Inflation can lower the value of your money over time. The real interest rate tells you how much your savings are actually growing after factoring in inflation.
Inflation vs. your money
First, let's remind ourselves. Inflation means that over time, the price of goods and services goes up. If you've noticed your grocery bill creeping higher without buying more items, you're witnessing inflation.
Now, think about the money you're saving. If you're stashing it under a mattress or in a regular checking account that earns little to no interest, that money is actually losing value in the long run because of inflation.
When savings don't keep up
Imagine you've saved
\[\$1{,}000\]
, and it's earning
\[1\%\]
interest per year in your bank account. Meanwhile, inflation is ticking away at
\[3\%\]
per year. While your bank balance at the end of the year shows
\[\$1{,}010\]
, that extra
\[\$10\]
doesn't go as far as you'd hope because prices for the things you want to buy have gone up by
\[3\%\]
. Essentially, you have less purchasing power.
Real interest rate: The true measure of growth
Your real interest rate is what tells you if your money is genuinely growing after factoring in inflation. It's simple:
\[ \text{Interest you earn} - \text{Inflation rate} = \text{Real interest rate}\]
This calculation can help you make smarter choices about where to keep your savings. For example, if inflation is at
\[3\%\]
and your savings account offers a
\[1\%\]
interest rate, your real interest rate is actually
\[-2\%\]
.
Always think of inflation as something that cancels the growth. It is the opposite of earning. So, in this case, even though you are earning interest, because the inflation is higher than your interest rate, your money is losing value.

Think of inflation as the opposite of interest: as interest tries to increase your money's value, inflation works to reduce how much that money can buy.
Your raise vs. inflation
Now, let's apply this understanding to your salary. Getting a raise is always exciting, but to truly gauge its impact, measure it against inflation.
Understanding your raise

\[4\%\]

\[3\%\]
, your real increase in purchasing power is only
\[1\%\]
.
So, in terms of what your money can buy, that raise gives you just a bit more than you had before.
It’s not to say a
\[4\%\]
raise isn’t good—it’s about understanding its actual value to you in an inflationary environment.

How to fight inflation
Knowing how your savings or raise stacks up against inflation is key. Here are some actionable steps you can take to fight the inflation:
Look for higher interest rates: High-yield savings accounts, CDs, or other investment options might provide better returns that could beat inflation.
Understand your total compensation: When considering a job offer or raise, look at the full package. Benefits like health insurance and retirement contributions can add significant value beyond just the salary increase.
Invest wisely: Investments in the stock market or real estate may offer returns that outpace inflation over the long term. However, remember there's risk involved, so it's wise to do your research or consult with a financial advisor.
Inflation doesn't have to be a scary word. By understanding how it interacts with your savings and earnings, you can make more informed choices that help keep your money's value strong over time.
