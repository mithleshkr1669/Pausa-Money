Government role in banking
Government rules help protect your money in banks, and agencies like the Fed and FDIC make sure banks are fair and safe. However, these protections don't apply to money in apps like Venmo or PayPal.
Banking basics: why we need the government to be involved
A long time ago, people had a tough time trusting banks. Imagine a world where you'd hear tale after tale about banks charging too much or slipping confusing terms into agreements. And sometimes, banks would collapse, taking all of their customers' hard-earned money with them. This made folks so nervous about banks that they figured money was safer tucked away under their beds than in a bank account.
But let's be real—stashing your cash in pillowcases wasn't the best or safest plan, especially if you ever wanted your money to grow. The government saw this and thought, "We need to fix it." They realized that without trust, people weren't going to use banks, and a country needs its citizens to use banks if the economy is going to thrive. So, government agencies were formed to oversee banks, and new rules were put in place to protect citizens and their hard-earned money.
Who looks after your money?
Here's a closer look at the agencies that monitor banks and what they do:
The Federal Reserve (Fed): They're like the guardians of the country's banking system and the entire economy. The Fed checks on all banks to keep everything running smoothly, loans money to banks to make sure they have enough, and decides on interest rates to help control things like how much things cost (inflation) and how many jobs are out there (employment).
Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC): This agency focuses on all national banks. They make sure national banks are following the rules and treating everybody right. When Wells Fargo got caught opening accounts without telling customers, it was the OCC that took charge. They fined Wells Fargo and made them agree to change how they do business.
Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB): These folks are your financial defenders. They make sure banks, lenders, and other financial types aren't being sneaky with you. When Citibank was not being clear about fees and billing practices related to their credit card services, the CFPB made Citibank pay back

million to customers. They also had Citibank fix their advertising and practices so that customers wouldn't be misled in the future. If you ever have a complaint about a banking institution or a lender, you would file it with CFPB.
Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC): Imagine your bank shuts its doors for good. That's where FDIC insurance kicks in – it's a promise that you won't lose all your money. They will cover your deposits up to
\[\$250{,}000\]
. This happened with Washington Mutual in 2008. They went out of business and the FDIC stepped in to make sure customers got their deposits back.
National Credit Union Administration (NCUA): It's the FDIC's counterpart but for credit unions. Just like with banks, they make sure your money is safe in credit unions by insuring deposits up to
\[\$250{,}000\]
. This means if something goes wrong with your credit union, you will get your money back.
Lastly, each state has state regulators that oversee banks in their state.
Knowing what government agencies do helps you see how they're working to keep your money safe at banks and credit unions. These regulators are like a security team—they make it less likely that a financial institution will mishandle your funds or mistreat you. And even in the off chance something does go wrong, you're covered and can have peace of mind that there are protections in place.

Caution with money apps
When you're using apps like PayPal, Venmo, Cash App, and Zelle, just know they're not the same as a bank. They don't have FDIC insurance, so your money is not protected. If an app goes bust, there's a security issue, or your account gets hacked you might not get your money back.
So when you sell your bike or concert tickets, and the buyer pays through one of these apps, it's a good move to transfer that money to your bank account where it's protected. Use these apps for quick payments, but do not keep your money in them.
Why it's important to understand this
Knowing about the government agencies that oversee banking is key for every savvy saver and spender. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) is especially important because it's the place you can turn to when things go wrong. If you've ever felt wronged by a bank or a financial service, the CFPB is where you can voice your concern. They're the folks who will take your complaint and investigate it, really giving you the power to challenge unfair practices.
And when it comes to your money's safety, you want to know the difference between keeping it in a bank, where it's insured by the FDIC, versus sitting in a payment app like Venmo or Cash App, where it's not. Arming yourself with this knowledge means you can make informed choices, stand up for your rights, and keep your money secure.
