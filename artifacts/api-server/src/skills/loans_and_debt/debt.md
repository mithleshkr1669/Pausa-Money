Debt and its causes
In today's fast-changing world of money, knowing about debt, what causes it, and its impacts can help people control their money instead of letting money control them.
What is debt, and how does it grow?
Debt is the money that you owe to others. You might borrow money for different reasons, like buying a car, going to college, or paying for a vacation. When you borrow money, you usually have to pay it back with .

Good debt vs. bad debt: Understanding the difference
When you hear the word "debt," you might think it's always a bad thing, but that's not true! There are actually two types of debt: good debt and bad debt. Good debt is when you borrow money to invest in something valuable, like your future. Bad debt, on the other hand, is when you borrow money for things that lose value or don't help you grow financially.
Good debt: Investing in your future
Good debt is the kind that helps you achieve your dreams, improves your financial prospects, and adds value to your life. Generally, good debt involves borrowing money to invest in assets that will grow in value over time, generate income, or improve your finances.
An example of good debt is educational loans. If you borrow money to go to college or pay for training, you are investing in your education, which can lead to a better job and higher income. That's why people call this type of debt "good" – because it helps improve your financial situation in the long run.
Another example of good debt is borrowing money to buy a house. A home is an investment that can increase in value over time, so taking out a mortgage (a loan to buy a house) can be considered good debt too.
Bad debt: Weakening your financial stability
On the other hand, bad debt is when you borrow money for things that don't help you grow financially or lose value over time.
For example, if you use a credit card to buy a new TV or a trendy outfit, that's bad debt. These items lose value quickly, and you might end up paying interest on them for a long time. If you had to borrow
\[\$1{,}000\]
for a new phone and paid
\[15\%\]
interest on it, you would actually end up paying more than
\[\$1{,}000\]
for the phone, which isn't a smart financial decision.
Ultimately, the difference between good debt and bad debt lies in how the borrowed money is used and its potential to improve your financial future. Good debt can easily turn into bad debt if not managed carefully, and bad debt can be turned into good debt by being strategic about its use.
Debt and debt repayment
When you need to pay off debt, you have a few options. You can pay off the debt with the highest interest rate first, or you can pay off the smallest debt first. Each method has its pros and cons, so it's important to choose the one that will work best for you.
Paying debt off
Sometimes, you might find yourself with more debt than you can handle. There are several ways to help you pay off the debt, and two of the most popular methods are the snowball method and the high rate method. Let's explore both of these methods to understand how they can help you become debt-free.
The snowball method
Imagine you have a snowball, and as you roll it around in the snow, it gets bigger and bigger. This is similar to how the snowball method works for paying off debt.
In this method, you start by focusing on your smallest debt first. You pay as much as you can on this small debt while making the minimum payments on your other debts. Once you've paid off the smallest debt, you move on to the next smallest, and so on, until all your debts are paid off.

Using the snowball method, you start by paying off smallest debt first and build your way through the biggest debt.

The high rate method
The high rate method is also known as the "avalanche method" because it focuses on paying off the debts with the highest interest rates first. This can save you more money in the long run because you'll pay less in interest.
To use this method, you list your debts from the highest interest rate to the lowest interest rate. You pay as much as you can on the debt with the highest interest rate while making minimum payments on your other debts. Once the highest interest debt is paid off, you move on to the next highest, and so on.

In the high rate method, you start by paying off the debts with highest interest rate. This method saves more money than the snowball method, but may take longer to see your first debt paid off.

Which method is best for you?
Choosing between the snowball and high rate methods depends on your personality and what motivates you. If you feel encouraged by seeing quick progress, the snowball method might be best for you. On the other hand, if you're more focused on saving money in the long run, the high rate method might be a better choice.
Remember, the most important thing is to make a plan and stick to it. No matter which method you choose, being committed to paying off your debt will help you achieve financial freedom and enjoy the things you love without the burden of debt.
Mastering debt: Key terms explained
Managing debt can be challenging, but knowing your options can make a big difference. In this article, we're going to break down several ways to manage debt, so you can choose the best path for your situation. We'll cover the basics of debt consolidation, debt collection, debt counseling, and debt settlement.
Take charge: What you can do on your own
Create a budget
Start by setting up a budget. This is your plan for how you will spend and save money every month. For example, if you earn
\[\$3{,}000\]
a month, plan how much will go toward rent, groceries, utilities, and debt payments. By tracking your expenses, you might notice that cutting back on dining out could free up more money for paying off your debt. To learn more about repayment strategies, check out our video on the snowball and high-rate methods.
Communicate with your creditors
If you struggle to make payments, reach out to your . For example, if your car loan payment is too high this month, call the lender and explain your situation. They might agree to adjust your payment terms, which can give you some breathing room and help you avoid debt collectors altogether.
Understand debt collection
Debt collection starts when you miss payments for an extended period, and your creditors hire a company to recover the money. Always verify the identity of the debt collector who contacts you to avoid scams. Let’s say you owe
\[\$2{,}000\]
on a credit card, and a collector now contacts you about it. You can sometimes negotiate to lower your payment amount or work out a payment plan. Remember, though, that having a debt sent to collections can impact your credit score negatively.
Consider debt consolidation
Debt consolidation means taking out a new loan to pay off several smaller debts, resulting in just one monthly payment. For example, if you owe
\[\$2{,}000\]
on three different credit cards, you could consolidate (bundle) them into one
\[\$6{,}000\]
loan. While this simplifies your payments, keep in mind that the loan might have higher interest rates and fees. You might also need to use your home as collateral.
Need help? Get help from others
Try debt counseling
Debt (or credit) counseling can provide help on managing your money and debts. Credit counseling is usually free or low-cost and you can find credit counselors at your bank, or local non-profit organization. A credit counselor can set up a debt management plan, negotiate with your creditors, and organize a single monthly payment plan for you. If you are unsure how to handle your debt, these folks are there to help you find a solution. They are risk-free.
Be careful with debt settlement
Debt settlement companies negotiate with your creditors to allow you to pay a lump sum that's less than what you owe. For instance, if you owe
\[\$10{,}000\]
, a debt settlement company may convince your creditors to accept
\[\$7{,}000\]
instead. These companies charge fees for their services and can be risky. They might advise you to stop making payments without guaranteeing a settlement, which can lead to debt collection, more fees, and even being sued.

Debt settlement companies often advertise themselves as helpful, but they typically charge high fees and are risky to use.
Bankruptcy as a last resort
If other methods don’t work, you might consider bankruptcy. This legal process helps you cancel or reduce your debts, but it can seriously impact your financial future. It’s crucial to explore all other options first and consult with a legal professional if considering bankruptcy. Bankruptcy will impact your life for
\[7\]
to
\[10\]
years. During this time you may be unable to get any loans, have trouble getting approved for housing, and face disqualification from some forms of employment.
A quick word on credit repair
You can't fix your credit score without paying off your debts. Some companies might promise quick fixes, but they cannot remove true information from your credit report. They can only remove mistakes, and you can easily do that yourself - no need to pay anyone. Improving your credit score takes time, but with consistent effort and patience, you can slowly make progress.

What changes your loan schedule?
Three things change how much you pay and how long it takes to pay off debt:

1. Loan amount (principal)
   If you borrow more, you’ll pay more each month or for a longer time. If you borrow less, it’s easier to pay off quickly.
2. Loan term (how long the loan lasts)
   A short loan means bigger payments but less total interest. A long loan means smaller payments but more interest.
3. Interest rate (APR)
   A low interest rate means you’ll pay less overall. A high interest rate means more of your money goes to interest.
   What about your credit score?

   A higher credit score helps you get a lower interest rate. A lower score usually means a higher interest rate. Just like in a video game, you want the highest score possible.
   A good credit score can help you pay less each month and get out of debt faster.
