What is estate planning and why is it important?
Estate planning is making a plan for what happens to your belongings, like money and property, after you die. Without a plan, there can be confusion and problems for those left behind.
What is estate planning?
Estate planning is the process of arranging and managing your money, property, and other belongings for the future. Estate planning is important in case something happens to you, like getting sick or passing away. While it may seem like something only older or wealthier people need to consider, it's actually important for everyone, including young adults.

Estate planning involves thinking about all the people and things that are important in your life.
Why is estate planning important?
Estate planning might seem like a grown-up, or end-of-life topic, but it's essential to understand it - even at a young age. Not only will you need to create a plan for your own belongings one day, but you might also be a part of someone else's estate plan, like a beneficiary. Being a beneficiary means that your family member or friend has chosen you to receive something special from their plan, such as money or a treasured item.
By learning about estate planning now, you'll be better prepared to make smart choices when the time comes for you to make a plan or receive a gift from someone else's plan. Plus, it's always good to be the one who knows what's going on and can help others understand too.
The goals of estate planning
There are many different goals that estate planning can help you achieve. Here are some of the main ones:
Taking care of loved ones: One of the most important parts of estate planning is making sure your family and friends will be okay if something happens to you. This might involve setting aside money for your children's education, providing for your spouse, or making sure your pets are cared for.
Deciding who gets your belongings: Estate planning helps you decide who will receive your belongings, money, and property if you pass away. This can be important if you want certain people to have specific things, or if you want to make sure your possessions are divided fairly among your family and friends.
Choosing someone to make decisions for you: If you become unable to make decisions for yourself, estate planning allows you to pick someone you trust to handle your affairs. This person might be responsible for paying your bills, making medical decisions, or managing your business.
Expressing your wishes: Estate planning gives you the opportunity to make your wishes known, whether that's your preference for medical treatment, your funeral arrangements, or your wishes for how your belongings are divided.
What does estate planning look like?
Imagine that Naira, a young woman with a growing career and a young daughter, creates an estate plan. She designates her sister to take care of her daughter and sets up a to provide for her daughter's education and living expenses. She also names her best friend as the person who will make sure to carry out her wishes and her brother as a person who will make medical decisions if she is unable to.
When Naira unexpectedly passes away, her family and friends are devastated but know exactly what to do. Her daughter is being taken care of by her loving aunt, her assets are distributed according to her wishes, and her healthcare and funeral preferences are honored.

Her family is left guessing about her healthcare and funeral preferences. Why you should start thinking about estate planning
Even though you might not have a lot of assets or dependents right now, it's still valuable to start thinking about estate planning. Estate planning could be as simple as deciding who will take care of your pet if anything happens to you. Or, it could be understanding your role in someone else's plan.
As you get older, your life will change – you might get married or divorced, have children, or buy property, and at that point it is important to have an estate plan. By considering your estate plan early, you can make sure you're prepared for the future and that your loved ones are protected.
So, start thinking about your own estate plan now, and remember to update it as your life changes. You'll be taking an important step toward protecting your loved ones, your legacy, and your wishes.
What are guardianship of minor children, wills, and beneficiary designation and why are they important for estate planning?
Estate planning is making a plan for what happens to your stuff when you're not around anymore. You might have learned about why it's important, but now let's talk about how to come up with an estate plan. We'll learn about wills, beneficiary designation, and guardianship of minor children.
Key terms in estate planning
Let's define some important words you'll need to know when dealing with an estate plan:
Will: A legal document that says who gets your stuff when you die.
Trust: A legal arrangement where someone holds your property for the benefit of others.
Beneficiary: The person who inherits your belongings from your will, trust, or life insurance.
Executor: The person who makes sure your will is followed.
Healthcare proxy: Someone who makes medical decisions for you if you can't.
Probate: A court process that makes sure your will is followed and your debts are paid.
Guardian: A person who takes care of your kids if you can't.
Guardianship of a minor child: The legal responsibility for taking care of and making decisions for a child under the age of 18, if you are not able to do so.
What does estate planning look like?
Let's look at estate planning in a real-life scenario:
Ali is a married father of two. He wants to make sure that his wife, Fatima, and their two kids, Amina and Yusuf, are taken care of if anything happens to him. Ali sets up a trust, which allows Fatima to manage his property and assets on behalf of their children. He designates Fatima as the beneficiary in his will, as well as the executor to make sure everything is carried out according to his wishes. Ali also names Fatima as his healthcare proxy in the event that he is unable to make medical decisions for himself.
Finally, he selects his brother, Ibrahim, as the guardian for Amina and Yusuf in case something happens to both him and Fatima. Ali knows that if he passes away, his will needs to go through probate, but he feels reassured knowing that he has taken care of everything ahead of time.

Estate planning flowchart
Estate planning may seem complicated, but it only involves a few steps, most of which are done by you. To start creating your estate plan, follow these steps:
Make a list of your assets (stuff you own) and debts (money you owe).
Choose beneficiaries to get your assets.
Decide if you want a will or trust and write it. Most forms can be found online.
Pick an executor to make sure your plan is followed. Let that person know you are naming them as an executor, and check that they are comfortable with doing so.
Choose a healthcare proxy in case you can't make medical decisions. Let your healthcare proxy know what your wishes are.
Pick a guardian for your kids, if you have any, and they are under 18.
Talk to a lawyer to make sure your plan is legal. Your work may offer a legal service, or you can get forms and instructions on how to file them online.
Update your plan if your life changes (marriage, divorce, new baby, etc.).
Conclusion
It's important to understand estate planning, even if you don't have a lot of assets to leave behind. You never know if you might inherit something from a loved one, or be named a trustee or executor of someone else's estate.
By learning about estate planning, you can make sure your wishes or somebody else's wishes are carried out after passing away.
What are power of attorney, living will, and medical directive?
Thinking about the future, especially worst-case scenarios, isn’t exactly fun. But here’s the thing: life is unpredictable. If something happens where you (or a loved one) can’t make decisions, who steps in? What happens with medical care, finances, or other important matters? That’s where legal tools like power of attorney, living wills, and medical directives come in.
Power of attorney: picking someone to step in
A power of attorney (POA) is a document that names someone to make decisions for you if needed. Think of it like a backup plan—if you’re ever in a situation where you can’t handle things yourself, this person (your “agent”) can take over.
This can include:
✅ Paying bills and managing your bank accounts
✅ Handling property, like selling a car or dealing with investments
✅ Making medical decisions if you can’t
There are a few different types of POAs, depending on what you want:
General POA – Covers almost everything, like finances, legal matters, and property. This is great choice if you are still capable of doing these things, but prefer someone takes over. The moment you are unable to do these tasks (like if you get really sick or injured), this POA ends.
Limited POA – Only for specific tasks, like signing a document while you're out of town.
Durable POA – Stays in effect even if you’re badly injured or very sick.
A durable power of attorney is especially helpful because it ensures someone you trust can step in if you ever really need it. This can be useful for aging parents, people with medical conditions, or anyone who just wants to be prepared.
A power of attorney only works while the person is alive. If they pass away, the POA ends, and their will (or state laws) takes over to decide what happens next.
Living will: making your medical choices clear
A living will has nothing to do with who gets your stuff when you pass away. Instead, it’s a document that tells doctors what kind of medical care you do or don’t want if you can’t speak for yourself.
For example, let’s say:

- You’re in a coma after an accident.
- You have a serious illness that won’t get better.
- You’re on life support, but there’s no real chance of recovery.
  Your living will can say things like:
  ✔️ I do want life support or I don’t want to be kept alive by machines.
  ✔️ I wish to donate my organs to save a life.
  ✔️ I do/don’t want feeding tubes.
  Writing this down means your doctors and family don’t have to guess what you’d want in a tough situation.
  Medical directive (advance directive): putting it all together
  A medical directive (also called an advance directive) is basically a package deal:

1. A living will (your medical choices in writing).
2. A medical power of attorney (someone you trust to make medical decisions for you).
   This means:
   👉 Doctors know exactly what care you want.
   👉 Someone you trust can make decisions for you if needed.
   It’s an easy way to make sure your medical care is handled the way you want it.

How to get started
Setting these up doesn’t have to be expensive or complicated. Here’s how to do it:
Use online legal services – There are many websites that can help you create these forms quickly.
Find free state-specific forms – Many states offer free POA and advance directive forms that you can download, fill out, and sign.
Get them notarized – Most of the time, signing these in front of a makes them official (you can find a notary at banks, libraries, or shipping stores).
Talk to a lawyer if needed – If your situation is complicated (like owning multiple properties or businesses), a lawyer can help you customize these documents.
The good news? Many people can do this for little to no cost. And once it’s done, you don’t have to worry about it again for a long time.
Summary
A power of attorney lets someone you trust make decisions for you.
A living will makes your medical wishes clear in writing.
A medical directive combines both in one simple plan.
These aren’t the most exciting documents, but they’re really important. Whether you’re looking out for yourself, helping a parent, or just planning ahead, setting these up now can save a lot of stress later.
