Grants, scholarships and student loans: What is the difference?
So, you’re thinking about college (or trade school, or any kind of further education), and now you’re wondering: how do I actually pay for this? Good news! There are a bunch of ways to cover the cost, and not all of them require you to pay money back.
Grants: free money based on financial need
Grants are need-based financial aid, which means they go to students who could really use the help. The best part? You don’t have to pay grants back.
Types of grants
Federal grants (money from the U.S. government)
Pell grant – This one goes to students with exceptional financial need. If you qualify, you get it. No competition, no running out of funds—it’s guaranteed.
FSEOG (Federal Supplemental Educational Opportunity Grant): Also for students with high financial need, but here’s the catch: each school gets a limited amount of FSEOG money. Once a school runs out for the year, that’s it.
TEACH grant – This is for students who are studying to become teachers and agree to work in a low-income school in a high-need subject. If you don’t follow through, the grant turns into a loan.
State and school grants
Your state or college might also offer grants based on need, academic achievement, or even what you plan to study. Check with your school’s financial aid office and your state’s education website.
Scholarships: free money based on achievement
Scholarships = free money that you don’t have to pay back. But instead of being based on financial need, scholarships usually reward academic performance, athletics, or other achievements.
Types of scholarships
Merit-based scholarships – You earn these through good grades, sports, music, leadership, or other talents.
Need-based scholarships – These go to students who have financial need (similar to grants, but more competitive).
Private/community scholarships – Businesses, nonprofits, and community groups offer these. Some have pretty random requirements—like being left-handed or an expert in duck calling (yes, that’s real).
Institutional scholarships – Colleges offer these to students based on grades, test scores, leadership, or even your major.
Watch out for scholarship scams!
Not every “scholarship” is legit. Avoid these red flags:
🚨 An application fee (you shouldn’t have to pay to apply!)
🚨 A “guarantee” that you’ll win free money
🚨 A scholarship that requires a “redemption fee” (that’s a scam)
🚨 A financial aid seminar that turns out to be a sales pitch
If it sounds too good to be true, it probably is!

Student loans: borrowed money that you must repay
Unlike grants and scholarships, student loans = money you have to pay back—with interest. That means you’ll owe more than you borrowed.
Types of student loans
Federal student loans (government loans)
Direct subsidized loans – The government covers your interest while you’re in school (only for students with financial need).
Direct unsubsidized loans – Available to all students, but you’re responsible for all interest.
Direct PLUS loans – These help graduate students and parents of undergraduates cover extra costs, but they require a credit check.
Private student loans (from banks or credit unions)
These loans usually have higher interest rates and fewer repayment options than federal loans.

Other ways to save money on school
Work-study programs
This program lets you work part-time at school to help cover expenses.
Tuition payment plans
Instead of paying tuition all at once, some schools let you spread payments out over time.
Military and veteran benefits
If you (or a family member) served in the military, programs like the GI Bill and Yellow Ribbon Program can help cover education costs.
Tax credits and deductions
Some of the money that will help offset your school costs will come through your tax return.
American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) – You can get up to
\[\$2{,}500\]
per year for tuition, books, and fees.
Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC) – This covers up to
\[\$2{,}000\]
per year for any stage of education.
1098-T and 1098-E forms – These help you lower your taxable income based on tuition and student loan interest.
The big takeaway
If you’re figuring out how to pay for school, start with grants and scholarships (free money). Student loans can fill the gap, but be careful—borrow only what you need. And don’t forget about work-study, tuition payment plans, and tax credits that can help you keep costs down.
