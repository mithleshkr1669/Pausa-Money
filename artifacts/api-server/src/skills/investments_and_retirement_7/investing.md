How to invest in your retirement account
An important question: **how should you invest your contributions?** You might be imagining picking individual stocks—companies you’ve heard of and admire. But while this can seem exciting, it’s not the best way to set yourself up for long-term success. Let’s break down how to make smart investment decisions that align with your retirement goals.
The power of diversification
Imagine you’re making a meal. Would you want only pasta and no sauce? Probably not! Investing is similar. Diversification means spreading your money across different types of investments—stocks, bonds, and other types—so that you’re not relying too much on any one of them. This reduces risk. If one investment doesn’t perform well, the others can help balance things out.
The easiest way to diversify is by investing in mutual funds. These are collections of investments bundled together, so you get a little bit of everything in one package. Instead of putting all your eggs in one basket, mutual funds let you spread your investments across hundreds—or even thousands—of companies.

Stocks can rise or drop sharply, but mutual funds typically remain stable. They do this by spreading investments across many assets, which helps keep things steady when one changes a lot.
Understanding risk tolerance
When you’re deciding how to invest, it’s important to think about your risk tolerance, which depends on your age and your comfort level with market ups and downs.
Younger investors: If you’re in your 20s or 30s, retirement is decades away. You can afford to take more risks because you have time to recover from market dips. This might mean focusing on investments with higher potential returns, like stocks. Mutual funds that match this risk tolerance will usually have words like growth, aggressive, or equity in their name.
Older investors: As you get closer to retirement, protecting your savings becomes more important. You’ll want investments with steadier, more predictable returns, like bonds. Mutual funds that match this risk tolerance typically have words like income, conservative, or balanced in their name.
The magic of target-date funds
Here’s where it gets even easier. If you are worried or confused about picking the right mix of investments yourself, you can use a target-date fund. These funds are specifically designed for retirement planning.
Here’s how they work: the fund automatically adjusts over time. When you’re younger, it invests more in stocks for higher growth. As you approach retirement, it gradually shifts toward safer investments like bonds. It’s like putting your investments on autopilot!
Let’s look at an example:
A fund called Vanguard 2045 is a target-date fund. The 2045 isn’t just part of the name—it’s the year you plan to retire! If you expect to retire around 2045, you’d pick this fund, and it would handle the diversification and risk adjustments for you.
This type of fund is a great choice for anyone who doesn’t want to actively manage their investments or stress about the stock market.

Why not individual stocks?
It can be tempting to invest in familiar companies. But this approach has risks. Many people end up buying at high prices and fail to diversify, leaving them vulnerable if their chosen companies perform poorly.
By contrast, mutual funds—especially target-date funds—take the guesswork out of investing. They give you the benefits of diversification and professional management, so you’re not putting all your money on one roll of the dice.
How much should you contribute?
When you set up your retirement account, you’ll be asked how much to contribute. Many experts recommend saving at least
\[15\%\]
of your pay. If that feels like too much right now, start smaller and aim to increase your contributions over time. Remember, every dollar you invest today has the potential to grow thanks to compound returns.
Bottom line
Investing for retirement doesn’t have to be complicated. By choosing a target-date fund, you can ensure that your money is invested wisely and adjusted automatically as you get closer to retirement. Think of it as a “set it and forget it” approach that keeps your financial future on track.
With a little planning and patience, you’re building a retirement savings that will provide for you when you need it most.
Investing in 529 plans, HSA, and life insurance
When we think about investing, our minds often go straight to retirement accounts like 401(k)s or IRAs, or maybe even a regular brokerage account. But there’s more out there! Non-traditional investment accounts—like 529 plans, Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) with investment options, and variable life insurance policies—offer some really interesting benefits that can help you reach specific financial goals. Let’s explore what these accounts are and why they might make sense for you.
529 plans: saving for education
A 529 plan lets you save and invest for educational expenses, like college, K-12 private school, or even job training programs. These accounts come with big tax advantages, making them a popular choice for families.
How does a 529 plan work?
You contribute after-tax money and select from investment options like mutual funds or age-based portfolios. Your money grows tax-free, and you won’t pay taxes when you use it for qualified expenses, like tuition, books, housing, or even a laptop.
Why are 529 plans flexible?
If one family member doesn’t need the funds, you can transfer the account to another—siblings, cousins, or even yourself. Plus, it’s not just for college anymore. You can use the money for K-12 private tuition, trade schools, and apprenticeship programs.
HSAs: investing for healthcare
A Health Savings Account (HSA) helps you save for medical expenses and offers some of the best tax benefits around.
How does an HSA work?
HSAs give you a triple tax advantage:
You contribute pre-tax money, which lowers your taxable income right away.
Your savings grow tax-free when you invest in options like mutual funds (once your balance hits
\[\$1{,}000\]
or more).
You can withdraw the money tax-free for qualified medical expenses.
This combination of tax benefits makes HSAs one of the most powerful accounts for saving and investing.
How are HSAs flexible?
HSA funds don’t expire. You can save them for years, even into retirement. They cover a wide range of healthcare costs, from doctor visits and prescriptions to over-the-counter items like sunscreen and bandaids. After age
\[65\]
, you can use HSA funds for any expense (non-medical withdrawals are taxed like income but avoid penalties).

Variable life insurance: combining protection and investing
A variable life insurance policy combines whole life insurance coverage with an investment component. It works well for people who want to protect their loved ones while growing their wealth.
How does it work?
Part of your premium pays for the insurance, and the rest goes into an investment account. You can choose how to invest —options might include stocks, bonds, or mutual funds. The cash value of the investment account grows over time, depending on how your investments perform and it increases your death benefit at the same time. While you are alive, you can borrow from the cash value at any time, for any reason, tax-free. If anything were to happen to you, your would receive the death benefit instead, which is higher than the cash value amount. Death benefit is also tax-free.

With variable life insurance policy, as your cash value grows, so does the death benefit.
Why is variable life insurance flexible?
The cash value builds over time, and you can borrow against it or withdraw money if needed for any reason. Just keep in mind that doing so can reduce the death benefit.
Why these accounts can help grow your money
These accounts give your money the chance to grow far beyond what you put in. Thanks to the power of compounding and tax advantages, what you put in can double, triple, or even quadruple over time. The earlier you start, the more time your money has to grow—and the less you’ll need to contribute to reach your goals.
