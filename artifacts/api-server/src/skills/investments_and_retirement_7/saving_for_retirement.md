What is Social Security?
Social Security is a key part of retirement planning for most Americans, providing income to retirees, disabled individuals, and survivors of deceased workers. While it’s essential, Social Security alone is usually not enough to cover all expenses in retirement. Here’s a simplified look at how it works and its impact on retirement planning.
How Social Security works
Social Security was started in 1935 to help Americans as they get older or become disabled. It’s funded by taxes which go into two main funds. The Old-Age and Survivors Insurance (OASI) trust fund pays benefits to retired workers and their families, as well as to the families of workers who have passed away. The Disability Insurance (DI) trust fund helps people who can’t work because of a disability. Together, these funds support millions of people.
Where does the money come from?
Social Security is funded by payroll taxes that you and your employer pay while you’re working. However, this money doesn’t go into an account for you. Instead, it’s used to pay benefits to people who are already retired or who need disability support. When you stop working and start claiming benefits, future workers will be paying the taxes that fund your payments.
How much will I get?
Social Security calculates your payment based on how much you made during your working years, focusing on the
\[35\]
years when you earned the most. The more you earn, the higher your benefit will be, up to a certain limit.
When can I start getting payments?
You can start collecting Social Security at age
\[62\]
, but if you wait longer, up to age
\[70\]
, your monthly payment increases. Claiming before your full retirement age (around
\[67\]
) lowers your benefit, while waiting until after increases it.
How Social Security fits into retirement plans
Know how much: Social Security typically replaces about
\[40\%\]
of your pre-retirement income. To cover the rest of your expenses, you’ll need additional savings, such as a 401(k) or other investments.
Choose when: If you need the money sooner, you can start at 62, but your payments will be smaller. If you can wait, you’ll get bigger monthly payments. This can make a big difference in how much money you get over time.

Waiting to claim Social Security until later ages results in higher monthly payments.
If you are married: Social Security also helps your spouse or partner. If they earned less than you or didn’t work, they might still get a benefit based on your earnings. After you pass away, your spouse or partner may be able to receive your benefit, which can help them financially.
Taxes: Depending on how much total income you have in retirement, you may have to pay taxes on your Social Security benefits. If your income is over a certain amount, up to
\[85\%\]
of your benefits could be taxed, so it’s good to plan for that.
What is happening with Social Security?
Social Security is facing some financial challenges. There are more people retiring than there are workers paying into the system. To address this, the government might:
Raise the retirement age: You may need to work longer before you can start receiving full benefits.
Increase payroll taxes: You might see more money taken out of your paycheck to support Social Security.
Lower future payments: The amount of money you receive each month from Social Security could be reduced.
These potential changes could directly affect your financial plans, which is why it’s important to save and plan for retirement beyond just Social Security.
Understanding Traditional and Roth IRAs
Saving for retirement is a significant financial goal, and understanding your options can make all the difference. In this article, we'll explore two popular retirement savings accounts: the Traditional IRA and the Roth IRA.
What is an IRA?
An IRA, or Individual Retirement Account, is a retirement savings account that you, as an individual, open yourself. This is different from a 401(k), which is an account that you get from your employer. The "Individual" in IRA highlights that you have the freedom and responsibility to open and manage this account independently, separate from any employment relationship.
Traditional IRA: Pay taxes later
When you contribute to a Traditional IRA, you can reduce your taxable income for the year. For example, if you earn
\[\$50{,}000\]
and contribute
\[\$5{,}000\]
to a Traditional IRA, you get taxed as if you only earned
\[\$45{,}000\]
(
\[\$50{,}000 - \$5{,}000\]
).
Immediate tax benefit: You don't pay taxes on the amount you contribute this year.
Growth: Your money grows over time without being taxed each year.
Withdrawals
After age 59½: You pay taxes on your withdrawals, but hopefully at a lower rate if you're earning less in retirement.
Before age 59½: You'll owe taxes and a
\[10\%\]

The Traditional IRA is called 'traditional' because it follows the thinking that people traditionally pay less in taxes during retirement. Why? Well, when you retire, you typically don't have a big salary anymore, and many of your major expenses are behind you—like paying off your house, cars, or supporting your kids through college. So, the assumption is that you’ll be in a lower tax bracket when you retire and can benefit from paying your taxes then.
Roth IRA: Pay taxes now
With a Roth IRA, you pay taxes on your contributions now, but your money grows tax-free, and withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.
Immediate tax payment: Pay taxes on your contributions upfront.
Tax-free growth: Earnings grow tax-free, and you withdraw them without paying taxes after retirement age.
Withdrawals
After age 59½: Both contributions and earnings can be withdrawn with zero taxes and zero penalties.
Before age 59½: You can withdraw the money you've put in (but not the earnings) anytime without taxes or penalties. Anything beyond the money you've put in will result in a
\[10\%\]
penalty and income taxes.
A Roth IRA can be a better choice if you expect your investments to grow significantly over time. Consider this: If you contribute
\[\$10{,}000\]
to a Roth IRA today and it grows to
\[\$100{,}000\]
by the time you retire, you won't pay any taxes on that
\[\$100{,}000\]
when you withdraw it. You've already paid taxes when you first deposited that money.

Understanding 401(k)s and Roth 401(k)s
When it comes to saving for retirement, knowing the specifics of a 401(k), a Roth 401(k), and an IRA can make a big difference in how you plan. Each of these accounts comes with its own set of rules and benefits, and understanding these can help you figure out which path might work best for your retirement journey.
What is a 401(k)?
A 401(k) is a retirement savings plan that your employer typically offers. It lets you save and invest for retirement with some big tax benefits. Here’s how it works:
You contribute pre-tax dollars, meaning you don’t pay income tax on the money you put into the account.
The money in your account grows tax-free—no taxes on dividends, interest, or capital gains as it grows.
When you retire and start withdrawing money (after age 59½), you pay income tax on both your contributions and any growth.
This is what people mean by “tax-deferred” growth. You’re not avoiding taxes forever, just pushing them into the future—often to a time when you might be in a lower tax bracket.
Why 401(k)s are so valuable
401(k)s offer a lot of advantages that make them a cornerstone of retirement planning:
Higher contribution limits: You can save more in a 401(k) than in an IRA.
Employer match: Many employers will match a portion of your contributions. This means that for every dollar you put into a 401(k), your employer adds a dollar, so
\[\$2\]
are going into the account. This is essentially free money, so always try to contribute enough to get the full match.
Automatic savings: Contributions come straight out of your paycheck, so it’s easy to stay on track.
Loan options: Some 401(k)s allow you to borrow from your account without penalties, although you’ll need to pay it back with interest.
How does a 401(k) compare to an IRA?
A Traditional IRA (Individual Retirement Account) is similar to a 401(k) in a lot of ways, but there are also some key differences.
Here’s how they’re the same:
Both allow you to contribute pre-tax dollars and grow your investments tax-deferred.
Both require you to wait until age 59½ to withdraw money without penalties.
Both have once you hit age 73.
Here’s how they’re different:
Contribution limits: A 401(k) allows you to contribute significantly more. For example, in 2024, you could contribute up to
\[\$23{,}000\]
to a 401(k), compared to
\[\$7{,}000\]
for an IRA
\[^_\]
.
Employer involvement: A 401(k) is set up and managed by your employer. An IRA is something you open and manage yourself.
Employer match: Many employers will match some of your contributions to a 401(k)—free money for your retirement! There’s no such match with an IRA.
Investment options: With an IRA, you have more flexibility to invest in almost anything. A 401(k) limits you to the investment options your employer chooses.
The Roth 401(k): a powerful alternative
Now, let’s talk about the Roth 401(k). It’s another version of the 401(k), but the big difference is when you pay taxes. It is very similar to a Roth IRA because:
You contribute after-tax dollars—so you’ve already paid taxes on the money you’re putting in.
The money grows tax-free, just like a traditional 401(k).
When you withdraw money in retirement, it’s completely tax-free—both the money you put in and any growth.
Choosing between traditional and Roth 401(k)s
How do you decide between a traditional 401(k) and a Roth 401(k)? Sometimes, the choice is already made for you if your employer only offers one type. But, if your employers offers both traditional and Roth 401(k), then the choice depends on your tax situation:
If you think your tax rate will be lower in retirement, a traditional 401(k) might make sense. You avoid taxes now and pay them later, when your rate is lower.
If you think your tax rate will be higher in retirement, a Roth 401(k) could be better. You pay taxes now at today’s rate and enjoy tax-free withdrawals later.
You can even contribute to both if your employer allows it.
The bottom line
Whether you choose a traditional 401(k), a Roth 401(k), or even pair it with an IRA, the key is to start saving early and take advantage of the tax benefits. And if your employer offers a match? That’s free money you don’t want to leave on the table!
The earlier you begin saving, the more time your money has to grow, and the closer you’ll be to a financially secure retirement.

What is a pension? 403(b)? Simple IRA? and others
You might already know about IRAs and 401(k)s, but those aren’t the only ways to save for retirement. Depending on where you work, there are other plans that could help you reach your retirement goals.
Pension plans: a steady paycheck in retirement
Pensions are like a promise from your employer to pay you a steady income during your entire retirement. The amount you get depends on things like your salary and how many years you’ve worked there.
The cool thing about pensions? Your employer usually pays for the whole thing. But they’re rare these days, especially in the private sector. You’ll mostly find pensions offered to:
government employees (federal, state, or local)
teachers (depending on the state)
military personnel
workers in public utilities or healthcare
Here’s a key idea: pensions are defined benefit plans, which means the employer guarantees you a specific payout - the benefit. This is different from defined contribution plans, like 401(k)s, where you put in money - the contribution, but your future retirement income depends on how your investments perform.
403(b) plans: for educators and nonprofits
If you work in a public school or for a nonprofit, you might have access to a 403(b) plan. Think of it as a twin to the 401(k) - looks and acts the same, just with a different name. With a 403(b):
you can save money from your paycheck before taxes.
the money grows tax-deferred until you take it out.
you can invest in options like annuities or mutual funds.
457 plans: for state and local government workers
The 457 plan is another retirement savings plan, but it’s mostly for state and local government employees. Like a 401(k), it lets you save pre-tax dollars that grow tax-deferred.
But here’s a twist: with a 457 plan, you can withdraw your money early—before age 59½—without paying a penalty if you’ve left your job. That flexibility makes it unique and helpful if you need access to your retirement savings sooner.
Thrift Savings Plan (TSP): for federal employees
The Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) is like a federal government version of a 401(k). It’s for federal workers and members of the military. What makes it stand out?
Low fees: You keep more of what you save.
Simplicity: You can choose between traditional (pre-tax) and Roth (after-tax) contributions.
Easy investments: There’s a small menu of carefully chosen investment options, so it’s not overwhelming.

How much to save: Is a million dollars enough?
Now, let’s tackle another big question: How much do you actually need to save for retirement? A lot of people think, "If I save a million dollars, I’ll be set." But a million dollars in retirement isn’t like winning the lottery—it has to last you the rest of your life.

How long you’ll live: Will you need your savings to last

Taxes: Don’t forget that withdrawals from accounts like a 401(k) or traditional IRA are taxable.
Market risks: Your investments might not grow at a consistent rate every year.
Social Security: This can help, but it’s unlikely to cover all your expenses.
What will your expenses look like?
Here’s another thing to think about: your expenses in retirement might look very different from what they are today. Some costs might go down, but others could go up.
Likely to decrease:
Housing costs: If you’ve paid off your mortgage or downsized, your housing expenses could drop.
Transportation: No more daily commute means lower gas and car maintenance costs.
Childcare and education: If your children are grown up, you’re done with these costs.
Taxes: Many retirees find themselves in lower tax brackets.
Likely to increase:
Healthcare costs: As you get older, you’ll probably spend more on doctors, prescriptions, and medical care.
Inflation: The cost of everything—from groceries to utilities—tends to rise over time.
Long-term care: Things like assisted living or in-home care can be very expensive.
Insurance premiums: Life insurance or supplemental health insurance costs often go up with age.
Conclusion
The big takeaways here are:
Start saving as early as possible. Even small amounts add up over time, thanks to compound interest.
Save enough to cover your future needs. The
\[4\%\]
rule can help you estimate how much you’ll need.
Keep an eye on your future expenses. Some costs will go down, but others—like healthcare—will likely go up.
Remember, saving for retirement isn’t about hitting an arbitrary number like a million dollars. It’s about giving yourself the freedom to live comfortably and confidently in your golden years. Start today, stick with it, and let compound interest do the heavy lifting!
