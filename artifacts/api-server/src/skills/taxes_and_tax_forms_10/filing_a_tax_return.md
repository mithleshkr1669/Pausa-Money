Getting ready to file
Filing taxes can seem tricky, but don’t worry—it’s easier when you know what to gather ahead of time. Think of this like packing for a trip: if you’ve got the right stuff, you’re ready to go. In this guide, we’ll break down exactly what documents you’ll need to make tax filing as simple and stress-free as possible.
What do you need to file a tax return?

1. Personal information for you and your family
   Let’s start with the basics. The IRS needs to know who you are and who’s in your household. Grab these essentials:
   Social Security Numbers (SSNs) or Taxpayer Identification Numbers (TINs) for you, your spouse, children, and anyone you’re claiming as a dependent.
   Birthdates for everyone listed on your tax return.
   It’s kind of like filling out a team roster—make sure all the details are correct! Even small mistakes can cause delays.
   Tax documents
   Most tax documents you will need are official tax forms. You will know these are official forms because they have a combination of letters and numbers (like "W-2" or "1099-G"). Income forms, like W-2s, show what you earned, while others, like 1098-T or 1098, help you claim deductions or credits. Many are paperless, so check your online accounts as well as your mailbox.

Screenshot of official tax document. 2. Documents that show your income
Your income is the backbone of your tax return. Most of documents that show your income will come in the form of official tax documents.
Some examples include:
W-2 forms: If you have a job, your employer will send you this. It shows how much you earned and how much tax they’ve already withheld.
1099 forms: These come in different types for different income sources.

Self-employment income records: If you’re a freelancer or run your own business, you’ll need to collect invoices, payment app records (like Venmo or PayPal), or bank statements.
Note: Not all tax forms come in the mail anymore. Many, like certain 1099s, are paperless! You might need to log in to your bank, investment account, or payment platforms to download these forms yourself. 3. Receipts for deductions and credits
Here’s where you can save money by lowering your tax bill. If you plan to claim deductions or credits, you’ll need proof of your expenses. Common examples include:
Medical expenses: Save those bills or statements.
Charitable donations: Keep receipts or letters from the organization.
Education costs: If you paid tuition, you’ll need Form 1098-T from your school.
Childcare costs: Gather records of daycare or babysitter payments
Mortgage interest: Look for Form 1098 from your lender.
Retirement contributions: If you contributed to an IRA, HSA, or 401(k), keep those records handy.
Remember, deductions and credits save you money, so don’t miss out! 4. Proof of taxes you’ve already paid
If taxes have already been taken out of your paycheck or you’ve made estimated payments, you’ll want to have records of those to avoid double-paying. Key forms include:
W-2 forms: These also show how much tax was withheld from your paycheck.
1099 forms: Some of these will show taxes withheld from other types of income.
Estimated tax payments: If you made quarterly payments, keep a record of when and how much you paid.
Property tax statements: If you’re a homeowner, you might be able to deduct these. 5. Other documents to keep in mind
Depending on your situation, you might need a few more forms, like:
Form 1095-A: If you bought health insurance through the marketplace, this form helps you claim the Premium Tax Credit.
Student loan interest (Form 1098-E): If you paid interest on a student loan, this might get you a deduction.
Alimony records: If you’re paying or receiving alimony, keep track of those amounts.
If you sold any investments or properties, like a house, keep those documents, too.
A simple checklist to stay organized
To keep things simple, here’s a quick checklist of what you’ll need:
Personal information: SSNs, TINs, and birthdates.
Income documents: W-2s, 1099s, or self-employment records.
Receipts for deductions/credits: Medical bills, childcare expenses, charitable donations, etc.
Proof of taxes paid: W-2s, estimated payments, and property tax statements.
Special forms: 1095-A for health insurance, 1098-E for student loans, or alimony records.
Final tip: Stay organized year-round
Save yourself a headache by keeping your tax documents in one spot. Use a folder (physical or digital) to store receipts, tax forms, and other records throughout the year. Then, when tax time rolls around, you’ll have everything ready to go!
By gathering these documents ahead of time, you’ll be able to file your tax return with less stress and maybe even a little confidence.
