How to choose your filing status and form
If you earn income in the United States, you’ll need to file a federal income tax return each year. Filing taxes is your opportunity to calculate how much you owe, claim deductions and credits you’re eligible for, and—if too much was withheld from your paycheck—maybe get a refund.

Common mistakes to avoid
As you start working on your tax filing, keep an eye out for these common mistakes:
Not reporting all your income: Did you have a side hustle or sell items online? You need to report all income, including small amounts, like interest you earn in your savings.

Missing tax breaks or credits: These can greatly reduce how much tax you owe. For example, if you qualify for the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), you could receive money back.
Filing late: The IRS doesn’t wait around. If you miss the April 15 deadline, you could face fines and have to pay interest.
Filing statuses: which one fits you best?
When you file your taxes, you need to choose a filing status. This helps determine how much tax you owe and what benefits you qualify for. The IRS gives you five options to choose from, depending on your personal situation:
Single: For individuals who are not married.
Married filing jointly: For married couples who combine their incomes on one tax return, which usually results in paying less tax.
Married filing separately: For married couples who choose to file separate returns, which might be beneficial if one partner has high medical expenses or if the couple is going through a divorce.
Head of household: For unmarried individuals who financially support a dependent, allowing them to pay less tax than if they filed as single.
Qualifying surviving spouse: For those whose spouse recently passed away and who are supporting a dependent, providing the same tax benefits as married filing jointly for up to two years.

Standard deduction vs. itemized deductions
When you file your taxes, you have two choices: take the standard deduction or itemize your deductions. Let’s look at how these work.

   The standard deduction is a set amount of money you can subtract from your income. You don’t have to list out expenses—just take the amount that matches your filing status. For the 2024 tax year, the standard deduction amounts are:

If your total itemized deductions add up to more than the standard deduction, itemizing can save you more money. Otherwise, the standard deduction is usually the better choice.
How to decide between the standard deduction and itemizing
Add up your deductions: List all the expenses you could deduct, like mortgage interest, state taxes, and medical bills.
Compare to the standard deduction: If your itemized deductions are higher than the standard deduction for your filing status, itemizing will save you more. Otherwise, stick with the standard deduction.
Think about your situation: If you had big expenses, like high medical bills or a large donation, itemizing might be worth the extra effort. If not, the standard deduction is easier and faster.

Key takeaways
Tax deductions and tax credits are great tools to save money during tax season. Deductions lower the amount of your income that you pay taxes on, while credits directly lower the taxes you owe. When filing your taxes, compare the standard deduction and itemized deductions to see which one works best for you.
And don’t forget about tax credits! They can sometimes save you even more money than deductions. If you’re not sure which options are best for you, try using tax software or ask a professional for help.
