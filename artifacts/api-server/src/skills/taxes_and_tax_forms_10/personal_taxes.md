What are tax brackets?
How often do you hear about tax brackets in everyday conversation or in the news, particularly around tax season? Phrases like, "I'm in the 30% tax bracket" might sound like a person pays 30% of their income in taxes, but it's not that straightforward. If it were, our tax returns would be a lot simpler! This article will break down what progressive taxation is and clarify what being in a particular tax bracket really means.
What is progressive taxation?
Progressive taxation is when the tax rate goes up as the amount of income goes up. Simply put, the tax rate climbs up as the income does. The idea behind this is fairness, even though it may seem like it is not fair to apply different rules to different earners.
Tax brackets
Think of tax brackets as 'steps' on the progressive tax ladder. They split income into groups, each assigned to a specific tax rate. If your income increases beyond a specific limit, you progress up to the next step or bracket. Only the amount of money that falls into that bracket is taxed at the bracket's rate.

Summary
Progressive taxation uses tax brackets, or income ranges, to ensure people pay a tax rate that aligns with their income. In the US, your filing status - whether you're single, married, or a head of household - also helps determine which tax bracket you fall into. When your income moves into a higher tax bracket, only the extra income in that bracket is taxed at the higher rate. So, even if you earn
\[\$1\]
more and it puts you in a higher bracket, the increase won't decrease your take-home pay.
