Tax forms
Forms like W-2 and 1040 help you report your income and the taxes you’ve already paid. Others, like W-4 and I-9, show how much tax should come out of your paycheck and prove you’re allowed to work. Knowing how these forms fit together helps you pay the right amount and avoid problems with the IRS.
Tax forms
You've just learned about different types of taxes, like sales tax, property tax, and income tax. One thing we all have in common is the responsibility to pay taxes on any income we receive, whether it's from a job or even interest from a savings account.
In this article, we'll walk you through the key tax forms you'll come across throughout the year. Unfortunately, there isn't a direct logical explanation behind the naming of these tax forms, as IRS simply used whatever letters and numbers were available at the time the forms were created. But, we can help create some fun associations to help remember them. A journey through the world of taxes typically begins with your first job, so let's start there.
Congratulations on your new job! Introducing the W4 and I9 forms
With your new job comes some paperwork, including two very important forms: the W4 and the I9.
W4
The W4 form helps your employer figure out how much income tax to take out of your paycheck. You'll answer a few personal questions like your marital status and if you have any kids.
No worries, though, you can update your W4 form anytime something in your life changes during the year and your employer will adjust how much taxes they are withholding.
I9
Now, the I9 form is all about making sure you're legally able to work in the United States. To fill it out, you'll need a proper ID like a driver's license or passport.
Not a job, but a side gig? Meet the W9
If you are working a side gig or working as a freelancer—or if you're starting a new job as an independent contractor—you might need to complete a W9 form. This form gives your taxpayer identification number (or Social Security number) to the person paying you, so they can report your income to the IRS.
January: Say Hello to the W2 and 1099 forms
W2 form
In January, your employer will send you a very helpful form called the W2. It tells you all about the money you made and the taxes pulled from your paycheck during the previous year. Make sure to keep it safe, as you'll definitely need it when you file your taxes!
1099 form
If you received any money from any other source, you will receive a 1099 form. This can be from your side job, contract work, or even from your bank for any interest you earned in your account.
January and February: Paying for college or a house? Here come 1098 forms
If you have any educational expenses or you're a homeowner, there's a useful form called the 1098. This form has the details on mortgage interest or college expenses and will arrive during January and February. 1098 form is very helpful because it can help lower your taxes.
April and the 1040 form: The finale of tax season

This timeline shows a typical order of tax forms you will encounter starting with your job.
As they say, all things must come to an end, especially when it comes to taxes. The grand finale for wrapping up taxes is usually on April
\[15^{th}\]
. This is the day by which you'll need to complete the 1040 form, letting the government know about your income and nailing down your final taxes for the year. Keep in mind, the 1040 form uses all the info you've collected from your W2s, 1099s, and other forms. Once you complete this form, you will know if you owe taxes, or if you are getting a refund.
Lots of people get their taxes out of the way even before the April deadline, but it's definitely the last call to get them done.

Your guide to key tax terms
Filing taxes can feel confusing with so many unfamiliar words on the forms. But once you understand key terms like gross income, deductions, and credits, everything starts to make more sense. Knowing these terms helps you read your tax forms confidently and see how they affect what you owe or get back.

1. Gross and taxable income
   Gross income is your total income over the course of the year. This includes everything from wages and tips to any interest, dividends or capital gains you earned. Right off the bat, you are allowed to subtract certain things from your gross income—these are called above-the-line deductions. That will give you what’s called your adjusted gross income.
   From there, you may claim certain exemptions and deductions. Once those exemptions and deductions are taken, the number you are left with is called your taxable income. You calculate your taxes based on that amount.
2. Tax deductions
   You may deduct certain expenses when calculating your taxable income. For example, if you made
   \[\$45{,}000\]
   last year and have
   \[\$5{,}000\]
   in deductions, you effectively pay taxes on
   \[\$40{,}000\]
   of income.
   Above-the-line deductions: These are subtracted from your gross income right off the bat; they include qualified contributions to retirement accounts, student loan interest, and healthcare expenses.
   Itemized deductions: Additional deductions are calculated using the IRS Form 1040 Schedule A. These deductions include certain medical expenses, charitable contributions and more.
   Standard deduction: If you choose not to itemize, you can generally qualify to take a standard deduction. This is available to most people, though certain restrictions apply, and the amount is determined by your filing status. You may choose to itemize if your itemized deductions would exceed the amount of the standard deduction.
3. Tax credits
   A tax credit reduces the amount of tax you owe the government dollar for dollar. With some credits, if your credit exceeds how much you owe, you’re entitled to get the difference back as a refund.
   One of the most popular credits is the Earned Income Credit (EIC), meant to reduce taxes for lower- and middle-income families. The amount of the credit is determined by your income and number of children.
4. Dependents
   A dependent is someone you support financially—for example, elderly parents or children. Claiming dependents may help qualify you for exemptions and credits. Children may qualify as dependents until age 19, or 24 if they’re full-time students.
   Older children or adults who qualify as dependents may still file tax returns, and may even be required to file if their incomes exceed certain amounts.
5. Exemptions/Allowances\*
   In the past, "exemptions" or "allowances" were like special money coupons that could lower the amount of your pay that was taxed. The amount you could lower your taxes by was based on number of people living in your home. But after new tax laws were passed in 2018, they said good-bye to these specific terms. Even though we don't use these names anymore, we still provide similar info through the W-4 form that helps the IRS figure out how much money to set aside from our paychecks for taxes. So while the rules changed a little, the overall idea is still pretty much the same: the number of people living in your home affects your taxes.

Taxes are calculated on the amount that is left over after all deductions (and exemptions*) are taken out. 6. Marriage penalty vs. bonus
Married couples can choose to file taxes together or separately. Depending on how much money you both make, and how that income is distributed, the amount you owe the government as a couple filing together may be higher or lower than if you file separately. If you owe more as a married couple, it is often referred to as a marriage penalty. If you owe less, it’s often referred to as a marriage bonus. 7. Tax brackets and rates
Because the U.S. tax system is progressive, different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. Income levels are divided into brackets with higher tax rates on higher brackets of income. Whatever tax bracket the highest dollar of your income falls into is known as your marginal tax rate. But because of the progressive system, your tax bill is likely smaller than that. What you actually pay is known as your effective tax rate.
\[^*\]
Term "exemption" is no longer a tax term, but refers to the fact that number of people living in a household affects the amount of taxes taken out.

How to read a W-2 form
Think of a W-2 form as a year-end evaluation from your job. It shows how much money you earned and how much tax your employer sent to the IRS. In this lesson, you will learn what the form shows, why it matters, and how to read it with confidence.
What's a W-2 and why do I need It?
When you work for a company, you earn a paycheck, and you have to pay some of that money to the government in taxes. Luckily, your employer can pay them a little out of each paycheck so you don't owe a bunch when you file your taxes.
You can think of your paycheck like a pizza. Before it gets to you, your employer sets aside a few slices for the government—one slice for federal income tax, one for Social Security, and one for Medicare. Those slices are called withholdings, and they help cover the taxes you will owe at the end of the year.
Your W‑2 form shows you how much you earned and how much tax your employer already sent to the government. You should receive a W‑2 from every employer you worked for that year.
The basics of a W-2 form
A W-2 form includes two parts: sections (A–F) and boxes (1–20).
The sections give your basic information, like your Social Security number, your employer’s ID number (EIN), and both of your addresses.
The boxes show how much money you earned (wages and tips) and how much tax your employer withheld for federal, Social Security, and Medicare.
This info helps you and the IRS track how much money you earned and how much tax your boss already took out of your income.

1098 forms
You have seen how many tax forms exist, and each one has its own purpose. In this article, you will learn about 1098 forms. These forms help you understand money you paid for things like student loan interest or mortgage interest. They also help you figure out your taxable income, which is a key skill in your financial literacy journey.
What are 1098 tax forms?
Simply put, 1098 tax forms are critical tax forms you receive from financial institutions. Receiving these forms is really good news for you. Here's why: these forms detail financial activities you've engaged in, like paying mortgage interest, student loan interest, tuition fees, or even making certain charitable donations.
Why is this good news? Well, it's because these activities can help reduce your taxable income. So, when tax season comes around, these forms can potentially help lower the amount of money you owe. It's a win-win situation: you've made important payments throughout the year and now, these payments might help save you money on your taxes. That's why 1098s are important. They aren't just pieces of paper, they are tools that can help you navigate the complex world of taxes.
Different types of 1098 forms
In the grand family of 1098s, we have several members. Each one plays its role in reporting different types of expenses:
1098 (Mortgage interest statement)
The most common one is the 1098 (Mortgage interest statement). When you pay home mortgage interest of more than
\[\$600\]
, your lender will send you this form. It details interest, as well as any points or mortgage insurance premiums you paid.
1098-T (Tuition statement)
Calling all students! If you, your spouse, or dependents pay tuition and related expenses to a university or any eligible educational institution, you'll get a 1098-T. Normally, these institutions report either payments received (Box 1) or amounts billed for qualified education (Box 2).
1098-E (Student loan interest statement)
For those with student loans, if you've paid
\[\$600\]
or more in interest on a student loan during the tax year, you're eligible to receive a 1098-E from your lender. This form helps you figure out your student loan interest deduction.
1098-C (Contributions of motor vehicles, boats, and airplanes)
Finally, for the generous souls who have donated a car, boat, or plane to a charitable organization, a 1098-C form is for you. It provides the detailed information needed to claim your donation as a tax deduction.
When and how are 1098 forms used?
Generally, these forms start appearing in your mailbox or email inbox by the end of January each year. They're sent to you by your lenders or financial institutions—essentially those you've had major financial dealings with over the previous year.
These forms only come into play if you choose to your deductions on your federal income tax return. The process is straightforward: you just input the information from these forms into your tax return.

1099 forms
During tax season, a unique set of tax forms pops up: the 1099 forms. "What are these documents?", you might wonder. Here's a simple guide that breaks it down for you!
What are 1099 tax forms?
At its core, a 1099 form is something you receive when you've earned money outside of your regular salaried job (remember 99 as 99 ways to make money). Think freelancers, contractors, or consultants. These forms detail that extra income, making sure you're aware of exactly how much you've earned and, more importantly, what you have to report to the IRS.
Different types of 1099 forms
Not all 1099s are created equal. Each one of them has its own purpose, based on the type of income it records:
1099-MISC (Miscellaneous income)
This form reports the amount a company paid you if you provided them with services but aren't their employee. It’s common for freelancers, independent contractors, or self-employed individuals. Here is what a sample 1099-MISC form looks like:

This form reports interest income earned in a bank account or other investment platform. If you earned

1099-DIV (Dividends and distributions)
If you've received dividends or distributions from investments during the tax year, you'll receive a 1099-DIV. This form gets issued when you have distributions of
\[\$10\]
or more.
1099-G (Certain government payments)
Received money from the government, like unemployment compensation or a state and local tax refund? It gets reported on a 1099-G form.
1099-C (Cancellation of debt)
If you're lucky enough to get some debt canceled or forgiven, expect a 1099-C form. The catch is that you usually have to include this canceled amount on your tax return as income.

Use of 1099 tax forms
When you get a 1099 form, what you're essentially seeing is how much you've earned in non-salary income during the year. What do you do with this? You include it in your total income when you're filing your tax return.
Let's walk through it: say you have a regular job, and you made
\[\$40{,}000\]
in salary. But you also did some freelance work on the side, and you made
\[\$10{,}000\]
from that, which you got a 1099 form for. Come tax time, you don't just report your
\[\$40{,}000\]
salary. You add the
\[\$10{,}000\]
to it, so now you're reporting a total income of
\[\$50{,}000\]
. Yes, you're paying taxes on a higher amount, but that's because you actually made more money.
Bottom line: Those 1099 forms are basically reporting extra income. And ignoring them can land you with penalties or fines from the IRS - if you received a 1099 form, so did the IRS. Always remember to add all of them to your total income—so that you're paying a correct amount in taxes.
